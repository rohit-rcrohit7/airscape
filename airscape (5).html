Airscape

A LIVING MAP OF THE AIR · GUIDE

What you're looking at

Airscape turns hourly air-quality data into a moving picture of the atmosphere over any place on Earth. Pollution shows as colour and movement: cool teal means cleaner air, shifting through amber to deep red as levels rise.

Values come from CAMS, the Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service operated for the EU. It is a scientific model that assimilates satellite and ground observations, sampled here at several points around your chosen location and blended into a continuous surface. Model values can differ from individual street-level monitors, especially right next to a busy road.

The layers

▦ heatmapA smooth colour surface showing estimated concentration everywhere on screen, including hotspots between sampling points.
∴ hazeDrifting particles. Their direction and speed follow the real recorded wind for the hour you are viewing; their density, colour and turbulence follow pollution levels.
— roadsMajor roads from OpenStreetMap, weighted as emission corridors that shape the hotspots — strongly for NO₂, mildly for particles. In England the weighting is derived from Defra's modelled road-noise levels, which track real traffic intensity; elsewhere it falls back to road class.
♪ noiseEngland only. The official Defra strategic road-noise map (Round 4, modelled 2022): annual-average noise bands in decibels drawn along roads, on a 10 m grid. While it's on, tap anywhere on the map to read the decibel value at that spot. This is a long-term statutory model, not live sound. Clearest with the heatmap turned off.

The controls

searchType a city, street address or postcode. Pick from the suggestions and the map flies there at the closest zoom available, fetching fresh data for that area.
⌖ locateCentres the map on you and keeps a marker tracking your position. Tap again to stop. Your location never leaves your device except to fetch map and air data for the area.
timelineDrag through the past 31 days hour by hour, press ▶ for a timelapse, or jump back to the present with NOW. Beyond the present marker the label turns to FORECAST: up to 4 days ahead.
hover/tapMove over the map to read the estimated value at any exact spot. Tap a sampling point in the side list to see its 48-hour trend.
windThe compass shows the real wind for the hour on the timeline — direction the wind is coming from, and speed. It is what drives the haze across the map.

Reading the colours

goodWithin or near WHO guideline levels.
moderateAbove guidelines; sensitive groups may notice effects.
elevatedUnhealthy for sensitive groups; consider reducing prolonged outdoor exertion.
poorHealth effects possible for everyone; sensitive groups should limit time outdoors.

In the UK a DAQI badge appears in the header — the official UK Daily Air Quality Index (1–10, low to very high), computed from current model values using Defra's concentration bands. Tap the badge to switch to the European EAQI and back. Note the official DAQI uses longer averaging periods (24-hour particles, 8-hour ozone); the value here is an hourly approximation.

1–3 · LowEnjoy your usual outdoor activities.
4–6 · ModerateAdults and children with lung problems, and adults with heart problems, who experience symptoms should consider reducing strenuous activity, particularly outdoors.
7–9 · HighAt-risk groups should reduce strenuous exertion, particularly outdoors; people with asthma may need their reliever inhaler more often. Anyone with sore eyes, a cough or sore throat should consider reducing activity.
10 · Very HighAt-risk groups should avoid strenuous activity. Everyone should reduce physical exertion, particularly outdoors, especially with symptoms.

Index bands and accompanying health advice are set by Defra/COMEAP — full details on the official UK-AIR DAQI page.

Pollutants & health

PM2.5 · fine particles

Particles under 2.5 micrometres — combustion smoke, traffic, wood burning. Small enough to pass from the lungs into the bloodstream; linked to heart and lung disease, stroke and reduced life expectancy. The single most health-relevant pollutant.

WHO 2021 guideline: 15 µg/m³ (24-h) · 5 µg/m³ (annual)

PM10 · coarse particles

Particles under 10 micrometres — dust, brake and tyre wear, pollen fragments, construction. Irritates airways and aggravates asthma and bronchitis.

WHO 2021 guideline: 45 µg/m³ (24-h) · 15 µg/m³ (annual)

NO₂ · nitrogen dioxide

Mainly from vehicle exhaust, concentrated along busy roads. Inflames the airways, worsens asthma — children living near major roads are particularly affected.

WHO 2021 guideline: 25 µg/m³ (24-h) · 10 µg/m³ (annual)

O₃ · ozone

Not emitted directly: formed by sunlight acting on other pollutants, so it peaks on hot, still afternoons and is often higher in suburbs than city centres. Causes chest tightness, coughing and reduced lung function during exercise.

WHO 2021 guideline: 100 µg/m³ (8-h) · 60 µg/m³ (peak season)

SO₂ · sulphur dioxide

From burning coal and oil — power generation, shipping, some industry. Irritates the nose, throat and airways; asthmatics are most sensitive.

WHO 2021 guideline: 40 µg/m³ (24-h)

CO · carbon monoxide

From incomplete combustion, mostly traffic. Binds to haemoglobin and reduces the blood's ability to carry oxygen. Outdoor levels are rarely dangerous but track traffic intensity closely.

WHO 2021 guideline: 4 mg/m³ (24-h)

Grass pollen

Grains per cubic metre of grass pollen, the main hay-fever trigger in the UK and much of Europe. Seasonal: peaks late May to July in Britain. Comes from the CAMS European model, whose domain covers all of geographic Europe including the UK — the Met Office does not publish an open pollen data feed.

Typical thresholds: low <30 · moderate 30–50 · high 50–150 · very high >150 grains/m³

Timeline & freshness

The window is live and rolling: each visit loads the most recent 31 days plus a 4-day forecast, and while the page is open the present edge refreshes every 15 minutes. It is never a frozen snapshot. Forecast hours are model predictions and carry more uncertainty the further ahead you look.

Data sources

Air quality & forecast: CAMS via the Open-Meteo air-quality API (hourly). Wind: Open-Meteo, real recorded and forecast values. Basemap & roads: OpenStreetMap contributors, dark style by CARTO. Geocoding: Nominatim. Road noise (England): Defra strategic noise mapping Round 4, © Department for Environment, Food & Rural Affairs 2023, used under the Open Government Licence. In Europe, CAMS additionally provides the EAQI index and pollen, which appear automatically where available.

In England, road emission weighting is derived from Defra's modelled road-noise levels (Round 4), which closely track real traffic intensity. Elsewhere it is an approximation based on road class. In both cases hotspot shapes near roads are indicative, not measured.
